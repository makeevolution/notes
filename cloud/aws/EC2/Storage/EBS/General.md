GP2: Based on IOPS Credits available at the moment of time in our credit bucket ^3OQrk8Tb

1. Our credit bucket max capacity is 5.4 million IO credits

If we do a 3000 IOPS operation, then, this means we 
transmit 3000 of 16 KB chunks of data per second

At this rate, this means we will exhaust our bucket in
5.4 million/3000 = 1800 seconds = 30 minutes

So you can sustain 3000 IOPS at 30 minutes until your bucket
is empty ^a0awv5J8

2. Every second, it fills with 100 IOPS credits

Additionally, we get 3 IOPS credits/second per GB of storage

So if our storage is 2 GB then we get 6 IOPS credits per second ^e1P7l7Ou

revise [[cloud/aws/EC2/Storage/General#^THo01IeIFyODC-naMbSb2]] for more info ^f4mD0Ibc

3. The storage is also performing IO with this rule
i.e. for 2 GB storage, then our storage will have a normal 
IOPS of 6

So, at normal use, the refill is used up directly ^PyoMrdbO

4. You can also use more IOPS from your bucket if needed
e.g. at an OS startup, the OS will require more IOPS

Max is 3000 IOPS
So our max throughput is 
16kb * 3000 = 48000 kb/s => 48 Mb/s ^QHyVSRpg

Max. possible throughput is limited at 250Mb/s by Amazon, based on 16kb block size (Which is the case with the IOPS) ^c8bOWsdA

5. If your storage size is > 1000 GB,
then the IOPS model is not used, and 
you always get 3 * StorageSize IOPS

e.g. for 3000 GB, you will always achieve
9000 IOPS
You can't go above this value ^L48dReKQ

6. Thus, given this limitation, then storages above 16000/3 = 5.33 Terabytes will output always 16000 IOPS  ^aQV9pWfv

GP3: Much simpler, you can use up to 3000 IOPS or/and 125 Mb/s as standard, regardless of storage size! ^44bg5yeW
